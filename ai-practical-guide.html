A Practical Guide

From Stack to Skill

Everyone is talking about AI, but few can place it. This guide maps the ecosystem you are building on, then turns to the more useful question: what does it take to work well within it?

Three parts ~12 min read Ecosystem · Fluency · Application
01 — The Landscape

The five-layer cake: where the value actually sits

It is tempting to think of AI as the chatbot on your screen. Jensen Huang, NVIDIA's chief executive, argues that this is like mistaking the icing for the cake. In his framing, AI is a five-layer stack, and each layer rests on the one beneath it — energy, chips, infrastructure, models, and, at the very top, applications.

The lower four layers are the largest industrial buildout in modern history: power stations, semiconductor fabs, data centres and the software that lets thousands of chips act as one. But Huang is emphatic about where the economic return lands. The value, he says, is realised at the top — in the applications people actually use, whether in financial services, healthcare or manufacturing.

Because the application layer is where value is monetised, it is also where you operate. Almost no one plants the tree — the roots and trunk are built by a handful of companies with enormous capital. But nearly everyone reaches into the canopy to pick the fruit. The foundation below is someone else's work; the canopy is the part you actually touch. It is worth singling out the four kinds of application growing there.

A

Assistants & copilots

Conversational tools that sit beside your work — drafting, summarising, analysing, coding. You stay in control; the AI accelerates each step.

Where it shines: collapsing the time between a question and a usable first draft.

B

Agents & automation

Systems that carry out multi-step tasks on your behalf — researching, executing, and chaining tools together toward a goal you set, rather than a single prompt you type.

Where it shines: work that is repetitive, multi-stage, or runs while you are elsewhere.

C

Domain platforms

Purpose-built tools for a field — drug discovery, legal review, financial modelling — where the interface, data and guardrails are shaped around one profession.

Where it shines: deep, specialised outcomes that a general assistant cannot safely reach alone.

D

Embedded features

AI folded invisibly into software you already use — the smart reply, the auto-summary, the "clean this up" button. No new tool to learn; the capability just appears.

Where it shines: reaching people who would never open a dedicated AI app.

The trunk is grown by a few. The canopy is where everyone else picks — which is exactly why the skill of reaching into it well is worth learning.

02 — The Skill

AI fluency and the four competencies

Knowing the ecosystem tells you what AI is. Fluency is about working with it well. Anthropic, with academics Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller, defines AI fluency as the ability to collaborate with AI effectively, efficiently, ethically and safely. Notably, it does not require you to code or to understand the mathematics underneath — those are useful, not prerequisites.

First, how are you engaging?

Before the competencies comes a simpler question: what kind of relationship are you having with the AI on this task? The framework describes three modes, and the balance of skills shifts depending on which you are in.

Mode 01

Automation

You scope a specific task and the AI executes it. You define the edges; it fills them in.

Mode 02

Augmentation

You and the AI work the same problem together, as collaborators passing a draft back and forth.

Mode 03

Agency

You guide the AI to act independently on your behalf — agents and autonomous loops that run with less supervision.

Then, the 4Ds

Across all three modes, fluency organises into four competencies. It is worth noticing that prompting — the skill everyone fixates on — lives inside just one of them. The other three are where most of the real work happens.

DDelegation

Deciding whether, when and how to involve AI at all.

Before you write a single prompt, you make a judgement call: is this a task to hand over, to share, or to keep entirely human? Good delegation is workflow design, not offloading.

  • Problem awareness — knowing what you actually need done."I need a first draft, not a final answer."
  • Platform awareness — matching the task to the right tool.a coding agent for a script; an assistant for a memo.
  • Task framing — splitting work into AI-suited and human-only parts.let AI summarise 40 reviews; you decide the strategy.
DDescription

Communicating the task so the AI behaves usefully. Prompting lives here.

Description is more than clever phrasing — it is giving context, constraints, examples and a clear sense of the finish line.

  • Product description — what you want back."A 200-word summary, plain English, for a board audience."
  • Process description — how to get there."Think step by step, then give me the shortlist."
  • Performance description — the role, tone and standards."Act as a sceptical editor; challenge weak claims."
DDiscernment

Judging the quality, accuracy and bias of what comes back.

Critical thinking cannot be outsourced, no matter how confident the output sounds. You treat every result as a strong draft, not a finished product.

  • Product discernment — is the answer actually right?spotting a confidently invented statistic.
  • Process discernment — did it reason soundly?noticing it skipped a step you asked for.
  • Performance discernment — does it sound like you, and meet the bar?"Correct, but the tone is nothing like our brand."
DDiligence

Taking responsibility for what you do with AI, and how.

Whatever the AI produces in your name is yours. Diligence covers the ethics, transparency and accountability that productivity must never override.

  • Creation diligence — using AI responsibly as you build.not feeding confidential data into an open tool.
  • Transparency diligence — being honest about AI's role.disclosing AI assistance where it matters.
  • Deployment diligence — owning what you release.verifying facts before the report goes out under your name.

Prompting is roughly one quarter of one of the four competencies. The other three quarters are choosing the right work, judging the output, and standing behind it.

03 — The Application

Application fluency: what proficiency actually unlocks

The 4Ds are the how. Application fluency is the what next — knowing, once you are competent, what today's leading tools can genuinely do for you. Introductory courses from the major AI labs converge on a similar set of capabilities. Stripped of any one product's branding, here is what proficiency unlocks, and why each matters.

i

Extended, context-rich conversation

Leading assistants hold long, coherent exchanges and keep track of what you have already said. Fluency means front-loading context and building on prior turns rather than restarting each time.

The benefit: the tool compounds — each turn is sharper than the last because it inherits everything before it.

ii

Working from your own material

You can bring documents, spreadsheets, images and data to the conversation. A proficient user stops asking generic questions and starts asking questions about their own files.

The benefit: answers grounded in your reality, not the internet's average — analysis of your numbers, summaries of your reports.

iii

Producing real, finished artefacts

Modern tools generate more than text: working documents, spreadsheets with live formulas, slide decks, charts and code you can run. Fluency is describing the deliverable precisely enough to get something usable back.

The benefit: you leave the conversation with a file you can send, not a wall of text you still have to build.

iv

Reaching out to the live world

With search and connectors, assistants pull in current information and act across the apps you already use — calendars, drives, inboxes. Proficiency means knowing when to let the tool fetch fresh data versus reason from what it knows.

The benefit: current, verifiable answers, and work that happens inside your existing systems rather than beside them.

v

Reusable set-ups and delegated tasks

The most capable tools let you save context, define standing instructions, and hand off multi-step tasks that run with light supervision. This is where you cross from assistant into agent.

The benefit: you stop repeating yourself — the tool remembers your standards and executes longer chains of work on its own.

Read these back against the 4Ds and the shape of fluency becomes clear. Delegation decides which of these capabilities a task needs. Description is how you invoke them precisely. Discernment is how you check what returns — especially with live data and generated files. And Diligence is what keeps the whole thing trustworthy as you hand over more.

In closing

The lower layers of the cake will keep growing — more energy, faster chips, larger models. But for most of us, the opportunity is not down in the trunk; it is up in the canopy, and it is a skill. Understanding the ecosystem tells you where you stand. The 4Ds tell you how to work. Application fluency tells you what is now possible. The people who thrive will not be those who write the cleverest prompts, but those who orchestrate human judgement and machine capability into a single, dependable partnership.

Frameworks referenced: Jensen Huang's "five-layer cake" of the AI stack (energy, chips, infrastructure, models, applications), as presented at the World Economic Forum, Davos 2026; and the AI Fluency 4D Framework (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) developed by Anthropic with Prof. Rick Dakan (Ringling College) and Prof. Joseph Feller (University College Cork), released under a Creative Commons licence. Application-fluency capabilities are generalised from introductory AI-assistant training courses and are described tool-agnostically.

Part Two · From Theory to Execution

From Skill to Practice

The first guide named the four competencies. This one turns them into a single connected workflow — four simple frameworks where each decision feeds the next, so a choice you make at the start still shapes what you check at the end.

Continues from “From Stack to Skill” — you don't need it first, but the 4Ds live there.
Four frameworks ~11 min read One handoff chain
00 — The Idea

Fluency is a relay, not four separate skills

Most people meet the 4Ds as a checklist: four things to be good at. That is true but incomplete. In practice they behave like a relay — each stage produces something small and specific that the next stage runs on. Get the handoff right and the whole chain gets easier. Get it wrong at the start and no amount of clever prompting downstream can recover it.

So the useful question is not “how do I do Description well?” in the abstract. It is: given the call I made at Delegation, what does good Description now look like — and what does that, in turn, tell me to check? That single thread is what this piece builds. Here is the chain in one view.

Four short frameworks follow — one per stage. Each ends by handing you the exact artefact the next one needs.

01 — Delegation

The Leverage Scan: find the work worth handing over

Delegation fails most often not because people pick the wrong tool, but because they never looked at their work as a whole before reaching for one. The Leverage Scan fixes that. It is a two-minute pass over any task or week that sorts work by a single question: where does AI create the most value for the least risk?

Run every candidate task through two axes. Leverage — how much time, reach or quality is on the line if this goes faster or better. Suitability — how well-matched the task is to what AI does reliably (bounded, text- or pattern-heavy, tolerant of a draft-then-refine loop). Plot the two and the quadrant tells you what to do.

The scan does one more thing, and it is the important one: for each task you keep, it records three facts that become the task profile you hand to Description. What mode you're in (automation, augmentation or agency), how much is at stake, and what "done" would actually look like.

Then pick the surface

Choosing to use AI isn't one choice — it's two. The second is where. The same task handled in a chat window, a delegated-work surface, or a coding environment gives you very different leverage, and the right pick falls out of the profile you just built. Read across from the mode and the shape of the deliverable, and the surface chooses itself.

Shape · a conversation

Chat assistant

You stay in the loop, turn by turn.

Thinking, drafting, analysing, quick Q&A. You supply context and steer every step.

Pick when: mode is augmentation and you want a collaborator you actively drive.→ Claude chat
Shape · a job to run

Agentic workspace

You set a goal; it works across steps.

Multi-stage research, analysis over many files, tasks that run with light supervision and hand back a package.

Pick when: mode is agency, or automation spanning many steps.→ Claude Cowork
Shape · code & systems

Coding environment

It reads, writes and runs a codebase.

Building, debugging, refactoring, shipping. The deliverable is software that has to actually run.

Pick when: the artefact is code — in any mode.→ Claude Code
Shape · inside a document

Embedded surface

The AI meets you in the file itself.

Work that lives in a spreadsheet, a deck, a doc or the browser — where leaving the tool would break the flow.

Pick when: the output is the document you're already in.→ Claude in Excel · PowerPoint · Chrome

The named tools are today's Claude surfaces; the four shapes are the durable part — any assistant, agent, coding tool and embedded feature maps onto them. Match the shape, not the brand, and the choice survives the next product release.

Delegation producesA task profile: mode · stakes · surface · a one-line definition of done.
→
Description receivesA clear task to translate into an instruction — with its mode, surface and finish line already fixed.

Choosing to use AI is really two choices: what kind of task it is, and which surface it belongs on. The scan settles both before you type a word.

02 — Description

The Spec Ladder: match how you ask to what you delegated

Here is where the chain earns its keep. Description isn't one skill applied uniformly — the kind of description a task needs is set by the mode you recorded at Delegation. A bounded automation task and an open agency task need very different instructions. The Spec Ladder maps mode to method so you stop over- or under-specifying.

If mode =Automation

Describe tightly. Precision beats freedom. The task is bounded, so pin the output shape, the format and the constraints. Lead with product description — exactly what you want back — and give an example of “good.”

"Extract every dollar figure from this PDF into a two-column table: item, amount. Numbers only, no commentary."

If mode =Augmentation

Describe the process, then iterate. You're collaborating, so set the role and the thinking method, not just the output. Lean on process and performance description and expect a back-and-forth.

"Act as a sceptical editor. Read my draft, then think step by step about its three weakest claims before suggesting fixes."

If mode =Agency

Describe the goal, the guardrails and the stop conditions. The AI will make choices you won't see, so specify success, boundaries, and when to pause and ask. This is spec-writing, not prompting.

"Goal: draft replies to these 12 emails. Never send. Flag anything involving money or legal terms for me. Stop and ask if unsure."

Notice that “prompting” — the phrasing everyone obsesses over — is only the surface of the automation rung. As you climb toward agency, description becomes less about wording and more about defining a contract: what success is, what's off-limits, and where the AI must hand control back. That contract is also, conveniently, your success test — which is exactly what Discernment needs.

Description producesA spec with a success test: the instruction, plus an explicit statement of what “right” looks like.
→
Discernment receivesA standard to check the output against — written before the output existed, so it can't be swayed by it.

If you can't state the success test while you're writing the prompt, you're not ready to judge the answer. Write the test first.

03 — Discernment

The Check Matches the Task: right-sized evaluation

The mistake at this stage is checking everything the same way — either rubber-stamping a high-stakes output or laboriously fact-checking a throwaway one. The type of evaluation should be set by the same two facts you've been carrying since Delegation: the mode and the stakes. The Spec Ladder told the AI what to do; now the same profile tells you how hard to look.

The first article split discernment three ways — product, process and performance. Here's when each earns your attention, and how deep to go.

AutomationSpot-check the product

The output is bounded and testable, so check the result against a known truth. Verify a sample, confirm the format, look for the confidently-invented number. You rarely need to inspect its reasoning.

Recompute three rows of the extracted table by hand. If they match, trust the rest.

AugmentationInterrogate the process

You care how it got there, because you'll build on it. Read the reasoning, not just the conclusion. Did it follow the method you set? Did it skip a step, or smuggle in an assumption?

"You recommended option B — walk me through what you ruled out and why." Then pressure-test that.

AgencyAudit the trail

The AI acted across steps you didn't watch, so evaluate the whole trajectory, not just the final artefact. Check what it touched, what it changed, and every point where it made a call. Sample the actions, not only the outcome.

Before accepting 12 drafted replies: read all 12, confirm none were sent, verify the money/legal flags fired correctly.

The rule of thumb: as autonomy rises, your check moves from the answer to the journey. Low-autonomy work lets you verify the product cheaply. High-autonomy work forces you to audit the process, because the product alone can hide a bad decision made three steps back. Whatever survives this check is the verified result you now have to stand behind — which is where Diligence begins.

Discernment producesA verified result — plus a note of what you checked and how confident you are.
→
Diligence receivesSomething you've judged fit to use — and now must decide how far you're willing to send.

Don't check every task the same way. Check it the way its autonomy demands — the answer for bounded work, the whole journey for autonomous work.

04 — Diligence

The Exposure Ladder: match diligence to who's affected

Diligence is where the chain closes. Everything upstream produced a result you trust for yourself; diligence asks a different question — who else is exposed if this is wrong, and what do you owe them? The single best predictor of how much diligence a task needs is not how clever the AI was, but how far the output travels and who relies on it.

So sort by exposure, not by effort. The further a piece of work travels from you, the higher the rung — and each rung adds a non-negotiable gate before release.

LVL 11

Personal & exploratory

Audience: just you · reversible

Drafts, brainstorms, learning, private analysis. A mistake costs only your own time.

Gate: a sanity read. Use freely; trust but verify anything you'll carry forward.

LVL 22

Internal & shared

Audience: your team · colleagues act on it

Team reports, internal memos, analysis others will build decisions on. Errors propagate to people who trusted you.

Gate: verify key facts and figures · be transparent that AI was involved so others can weight it.

LVL 33

External & attributed

Audience: customers, public · your name on it

Client deliverables, published content, anything carrying your or your organisation's reputation. Errors become your errors, publicly.

Gate: full fact-check · human sign-off · no confidential data used · disclosure where it matters.

LVL 44

Regulated & consequential

Audience: government, industry, safety-critical

Regulatory filings, legal, medical, financial advice, anything where a person could be harmed or a law engaged.

Gate: everything above, plus qualified expert review · documented accountability · AI treated as assistant, never author of record.

The ladder makes the hardest judgement — “is this a big deal?” — mechanical. You don't estimate risk in the abstract; you ask a concrete question: who receives this? The answer picks the rung, and the rung names the gate. And because you tagged the stakes back at Delegation, a Level 3 or 4 task should never surprise you here — you'll have seen it coming from the very first step.

Diligence producesA release decision you can defend — the right gate cleared for the right audience.

Feeds back to DelegationWhat you learned about stakes and effort sharpens the next Leverage Scan. The relay becomes a loop.
05 — Try It

The workflow router: make one call, watch it cascade

This is the whole argument in one control. Make the two decisions you'd make at Delegation — pick a mode and an exposure level — and the frameworks resolve into a single, task-specific plan: the surface to reach for, how to describe, how to check, and what gate to clear. Change either input and watch every downstream stage update. That propagation is the point: the frameworks are connected, not separate.

06 — End to End

One task, all four stages

To see the handoffs carry, follow a single ordinary task the whole way down the chain.

The task

“Summarise 40 customer reviews into a one-page insight brief for next week's leadership meeting.”

01 Delegation

Leverage Scan: high value, strong fit → Automate first. Mode = augmentation. Surface = chat (you'll drive the angle). Stakes = internal. Profile handed over.

02 Description

Augmentation rung: set the role and process. “Cluster the reviews into themes, show me the clusters, then we'll pick the three worth briefing.” Success test written.

03 Discernment

Augmentation → interrogate the process. Read the clustering logic; trace two quotes back to real reviews to catch invented sentiment. Verified.

04 Diligence

Exposure = internal/shared → Level 2. Gate: verify the headline figures, note AI helped draft it. No client data leaves. Cleared to send.

No stage was done in isolation. The augmentation call at Delegation is why Description set a process and why Discernment read the reasoning. The internal audience is why Diligence stopped at a Level 2 gate rather than a full external fact-check. Change the task to “publish these insights in our public quarterly report” and only two inputs move — exposure jumps to Level 3 — but the whole downstream plan tightens automatically. That's the relay working.

In closing

The first guide argued that prompting is a quarter of one competency, and that the real skill is orchestration. This is what orchestration looks like up close: not four things you're separately good at, but one chain you run deliberately — a call at the top that sets the ask, the check and the gate all the way down. Master the handoffs and you stop treating each task as a fresh puzzle. You just run the relay, and it carries you from a rough idea to a result you can stand behind.

01 — The Framework

Two questions, and the room picks itself

Article two put Delegation first because it runs before you type a word. Here's the last thing it owes you: where the work happens. Four products looks like a menu to memorise — don't bother. They're four points on two axes, and the axes are the durable bit.

First: how long does the context need to live? Some work is a hotel room. Bring what you need, use it, hand back the key. Other work is an office — same desk, same files, every week — and setting it up from scratch each morning would be daft.

Second: who actually does the work? Sometimes you're in the passenger seat with a map, calling every turn. Sometimes you give the driver an address and read your book. One is a conversation. The other is a job.

One thing that trips people up: Code sits in the bottom half, and not because programming is some loftier kind of thinking. It's there because code is the only thing on this page that can be handed back and tested. A paragraph can only be read and judged; a script either runs or it doesn't. So if what you want at the end is something that has to work, use the room with a workbench in it. That's about the output, not about you.

Pick the wrong room and you pay for it all session — re-pasting background that should have just been sitting there, or hovering over a job that would have run fine without you.

Choosing to use AI is really two choices. What kind of work this is, and where that kind of work belongs.

02 — The Surfaces

What each one is actually for

Read the triggers rather than the names — the trigger is what you'll actually recognise in the moment, usually about thirty seconds too late.

Chat Claude Chat
Shape · a conversation you drive

The default, and rightly so — for work that starts and finishes in one go.

Chat is thinking out loud with something that thinks back. You bring the material, you call each turn, and the value builds inside the one thread. Its limitation is just the flip side of that: close the window and the context goes with it. Which is fine — most conversations shouldn't follow you home.

  • TriggerOne sitting, one thread. Everything the task needs fits in your hands on the way in, and you won't want it again next Tuesday.
  • ProficiencyLoad up before you ask. "Here's the situation and three constraints — don't answer yet, tell me what else you'd need." One good opening turn beats a clever tenth one, every time.
  • Failure modeUsing it for the same job every week, and paying the re-explanation tax on each pass.

Scenario. Board paper to get through before tomorrow. Paste it in, ask what a sceptical director would go after, argue with the answer a bit, walk away with sharper notes. None of that needs to survive the night.

Projects Claude Projects
Shape · a standing brief

Where context stops being something you carry in and starts being something that's already there.

A Project is a room with two things bolted down: a set of files, and standing instructions that apply to every conversation you have in it. Set the background once, and every new chat you open inside walks in already briefed. It's the difference between introducing yourself each time and simply being known.

  • TriggerThe third time you paste the same background in. That's it. That's the signal, and it's a dependable one.
  • ProficiencyKnowing what's shared and what isn't. Chats in a Project share the files and instructions, but they can't see each other's history — like colleagues briefed from the same pack who haven't compared notes. So you can run several threads at once without them bleeding into each other.
  • Failure modeLetting it become a junk drawer. Stale files genuinely make the answers worse — treat it as a briefing pack you keep tidy, not a shared drive you keep adding to.

Scenario. A client you'll be working with all year. Their strategy, brand guide and last four reports sit in the knowledge base; the instructions carry their tone and the handful of things you must never claim on their behalf. Monday's pricing note and Thursday's campaign review are separate chats, and both know the client from the first sentence.

Cowork Claude Cowork
Shape · a job you hand over

The real jump — from a conversation you drive to work that carries on without you.

This is where you stop typing to a chatbot and start briefing a capable temp. You point Cowork at a folder, tell it what you want, and it plans the steps, works through your actual files and hands back something finished. It shows you the plan before it starts, and you can lean in and redirect at any point — but the posture is different. You're reviewing the work, not watching the working. Sessions keep going when you shut the laptop, and you can put jobs on a schedule.

  • TriggerLots of steps, lots of files, and no reason for you to watch. If explaining it takes less time than doing it, and the steps are mechanical, hand it over.
  • ProficiencyBriefing someone who can't tap you on the shoulder. Say what "done" looks like, what to do when something's ambiguous, and what to leave well alone. Then actually read the plan before you approve it — cheapest checkpoint you'll ever get.
  • Failure modeHanding over something you can't check. A wrong assumption in a conversation gets caught on the next turn; a wrong assumption in a job compounds quietly through forty files before you see any of it.

Scenario. A year of expense receipts in a folder, wanted as a categorised spreadsheet with quarterly totals. You describe the output, spell out the rules for the odd ones, approve the plan and go to your meeting. You come back to a file — and your job is checking it rather than building it.

Code Claude Code
Shape · something that has to run

The one deliverable that can prove itself right.

When what you want is software, you want a room where it can be run and tested. Code works straight against a codebase — writing, running, breaking, fixing. And non-developers end up here more than they'd guess, because a fair chunk of what we call analysis is really a script wearing a spreadsheet's clothes.

  • TriggerThe output has to run. Scripts, data pipelines, anything where "does this work?" has a real answer rather than an opinion.
  • ProficiencyLetting the machine mark its own homework — properly. Ask for tests alongside the code, then judge it on whether it runs and gets the right answer, not on whether it reads convincingly. Plausible-looking code is the whole trap.
  • Failure modeAccepting code you've no way of assessing. If you can't tell working from broken, you've handed over the judgement as well — which is exactly what Discernment warns about.

Scenario. That monthly reconciliation between two systems you've been doing by hand. Described once as a script, it turns into something that runs in seconds — and unlike an answer in a chat, you can point it at a month you already did correctly and see whether it agrees with you.

Working it through

You don't pay for this at the prompt. You pay for it in the room you walked into before you started typing.

04 — The Consequence

Where you work changes the other three Ds

This is what makes picking a room a skill rather than a preference. The choice doesn't stay put inside Delegation — it runs downhill. What you describe, what you check, and what you're on the hook for all shift depending on where the work happens.

D2

Description changes shape

In chat you describe as you go, adjusting when you see what comes back. In Cowork you describe once, up front, to someone who can't put their head round the door and check — that's a written brief, not a prompt. In a Project, most of the describing already happened when you set it up, which is exactly why the prompts inside can be so short.

In practice: the further you step back, the more your description has to anticipate instead of react.

D3

Discernment starts checking something else

In chat you're judging an answer. In Cowork you're judging the finished thing and how it got there, because a mistake in step two quietly infects everything after it. In Code you're judging whether it runs and returns the right result — the one place on this page where checking is genuinely objective rather than a matter of taste.

In practice: the moment you stop watching each turn, you have to start reading the trail.

D4

Diligence climbs with autonomy

A chat gives you text, and you read it before it goes anywhere near anyone. An agent reads and writes real files on your actual machine, which turns scope from an efficiency question into a safety one. Which folder it can see, whether the plan is sound, what a scheduled job is allowed to touch — those are all Diligence calls, and they land earlier than people expect.

In practice: the further you delegate, the sooner you have to decide what you're willing to answer for.

Read those three together and there's a tidy rule in it. Moving right across the matrix buys you persistence and costs you tidying. Moving down buys you leverage and costs you checking. Both are good trades — as long as you know you're making them, which is really the whole argument.

In closing

Same thread through all three pieces, really. The first mapped the ecosystem and found the value sitting in the application layer, which is where you work. The second argued that working there well is a set of competencies rather than a knack for phrasing things. This one lands on the most practical bit of the first competency: knowing which room to walk into. It's a small call, made before anything visible happens, and it quietly decides how much of the rest goes smoothly. Which is the slightly deflating punchline of the whole series — proficiency turns out to look less like knowing what to say, and more like knowing where to go and say it.
